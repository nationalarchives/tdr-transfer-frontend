About Transfer Digital Records

Transfer Digital Records (TDR) is a secure digital transfer service from The National Archives (TNA) that helps Public Record Bodies prepare and transfer selected digital public records to TNA for permanent preservation and publication.

Under the 20-year rule, transferring bodies must transfer selected and sensitivity-reviewed digital Public Records within 20 years of the date of the record, or the last modified date where this has not been set. This is usually determined by the record's date last modified. Until then, you must safeguard the records and actively manage their digital continuity to ensure you can find, open, understand and trust them over time and through change.

We are very happy to accept early transfers of digital records and encourage you to transfer digital records prior to the 20-year deadline. An advantage of transferring early is that once records are transferred, you will no longer need to manage their digital continuity. We also advise taking a 'little and often' approach to transferring digital records to The National Archives.

The current TDR service

We have created a product which provides a preparation area where records are uploaded to prepare for transfer to TNA. When uploaded to TDR, the records are still owned by the transferring body. While your records are in TDR, you will still be responsible for handling any requests such as Freedom Of Information (FOI). The records and their legal custody are only transferred to TNA during the final stage of the TDR process.

TDR is currently in beta, an early agile stage of development where limited features are available. This means it does not yet offer the full range of features that we intend to support.

At this stage of development, the service is only available to pre-approved users. Over time, TDR will be made available to more Public Record Bodies and will continue to be developed in consultation with our users.

Before you begin

Currently we can only accept records that are Crown Copyright, Public Records and from shared drives.

Over time, we will extend the service to accept a wider range of records.

Appraisal, Selection and Sensitivity Review

Transferring departments have a statutory responsibility to appraise, select and sensitivity review records prior to transferring records to The National Archives.

Any records not identified as 'closed' will be open to the public on transfer to The National Archives.

If there are any issues detected with the appraisal, selection and sensitivity review of records, The National Archives reserves the right to pause the transfer and refer to the Strategic Compliance team.

Secured access

In order to keep the service and your account secure, TDR uses Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA). This means you must sign in each time using a unique generated 'one-time password' (OTP) code that confirms who you are. This can be set up using a free-to-download authenticator app on your smartphone, such as Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator, or FreeOTP. Other authenticators are also available.

Free-to-download apps are available from the App Store for iPhone, or 'Google Play' and 'Play Store' for Android.

File formats

To ensure that records can be successfully uploaded and pass TDR validation checks, read the following information about the file formats we accept before proceeding to upload your records:

  • We cannot accept files that are password protected or encrypted
  • During beta, we cannot accept zip files
  • We cannot accept files that contain slashes (/ and \) in the name
  • Files such as thumbnail images (thumbs.db), executable files (.exe) and system files are not usually selected for permanent preservation. We will accept these but strongly advise that you remove them prior to upload.
  • If uploaded, empty folders will not be transferred

To identify file formats, search your folder for the file extensions (thumbs.db, .exe and .zip). For password protected files, click to view folder options and select ‘hidden files and folders’. These files are usually identified as part of your selection and sensitivity review process.

We recommend that you check your files prior to upload to ensure that you upload only selected records intended for preservation. During the upload and checking process, if we detect files we cannot accept, you will be notified on screen and you will be unable to proceed with your transfer.

  1. TDR runs a series of checks on the files to ensure they can be transferred. The following checks are performed:
    • Antivirus

      • A check performed to scan for viruses and other malware. We cannot accept infected files.

    • File format identification

      • A check performed to identify the file format and confirm that the file is suitable for transfer. Knowing the format also aids TNA with preservation decisions about the record.

    • Data integrity validation

      • A check performed to ensure files have not been corrupted as they move between your shared drive and TDR during the upload process. The data integrity check uses checksums which are like a digital fingerprint. For TDR we use SHA256.

Descriptive metadata

You are not required to add descriptive metadata to your records, but it can enhance them.

Once you select the file or files you wish to add descriptive metadata to, you may provide the following information:

  • Description – this will be visible to the public to help explain the content of your file or files
  • Date of record – this is prepopulated and shows the date last modified
  • Language

Closure metadata

If you wish to transfer closed files and folders, you must add closure metadata.

Once you select the file or files to add closure metadata to, you must first confirm the closure status has been approved by the Advisory Council. If it has been approved, you may proceed to add the following information:

  • FOI decision asserted – this is the date of the Advisory Council approval
  • Closure start date – this is the date of the last meaningful change to the record
  • Closure period – this is the number of years the record is closed from the closure start date
  • FOI exemption code – you should search and select at least one exemption code per file
  • Title sensitivity – you should confirm if the title is sensitive to the public. If it is, you can add an alternative title
  • Description Sensitivity – you can only provide an alternative description if you have set a description on the Descriptive Metadata page first

Titles and descriptions that are sensitive to the public

If the title or description of your record is sensitive to the public, you can provide an alternative when adding closure metadata. The alternative will be visible on the public catalogue. You may wish to do one of the following:

Redact – remove the sensitive words. For example: ‘Correspondence with [name withheld]’

Sanitise – replace the sensitive word. For example: 'UK military exercises in xxx' (where 'military' has replaced 'Special Forces').

Withhold the title or description without providing an alternative. For example: '[Title withheld]' and or '[description with held]'.

Glossary

Each of the following terms is defined within the context of Transfer Digital Records (TDR).

Anti-virus scan

A check performed on digital files to detect the presence of computer viruses or other malware. We do not accept files that contain viruses or malware and so if these are detected the upload will fail.

Beta

The Transfer Digital Records service is still being developed and is currently at a 'beta' stage. At this stage in its development, TDR does not yet offer the full range of features which our users require (for example, TDR can currently only accept records which are Crown Copyright and Public Records). The service is currently only available to an invited group of users.

Checksums

The integrity check uses checksums which are like digital fingerprints used to verify that no changes have been made to the data.

The checksum hash we use is SHA 256.

Consignment

A consignment is a group of selected records being prepared and transferred to The National Archives. Sometimes referred to as a batch.

Consignment reference

Each consignment of digital records uploaded to TDR for transfer to The National Archives is assigned a consignment reference which can be identified as a series of letters and numbers. Quote this reference if you need to contact us about your records transfer.

Creating body

The government department or body that originally created the records being transferred. This is often the same as the body that transfers the records.

Crown Copyright

Under section 163 of the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988, Crown Copyright is defined as works made by officers or servants of the Crown in the course of their duties. This includes material created by ministers, civil servants and government departments and agencies. Copyright can also be assigned or transferred to the Crown by the legal owner of the copyright. Most, though not all, Public Records are Crown Copyright.

Data integrity check

When you upload your digital records to TDR, we carry out data integrity checks to help ensure that files have not been corrupted during the upload process.

Departmental Records Officer

A Departmental Records Officer (DRO) is a person appointed by a government department or agency as being responsible for the management of the records of that organisation. The DRO must authorise any transfer of records to The National Archives and is responsible for ensuring that the records are properly sensitivity reviewed prior to transfer.

Export

Once your records have been uploaded and checked, your records and legal custody is then given to TNA where they will be preserved.

Freedom of Information (FOI) exemption

FOI exemptions exist to protect information that should not be disclosed. A list of exemptions can be found in Part II of the Freedom of Information Act, at sections 21 to 44. Records transferred to The National Archives will be released to the public (‘opened’) unless you apply one or more FOI exemptions.

Metadata

Data that provides additional information about digital records. For example, information that could help a user find or understand a record, locate other related information or understand how the record was originally created or used.

Public Records

Records of, or held in, any department of Her Majesty’s Government in the United Kingdom or records of any office, commission or other body or establishment whatsoever under Her Majesty’s Government in the United Kingdom, as defined in paragraph 2 of the First Schedule to the Public Records Act 1958.

Most records held by the UK central government and related bodies are Public Records.

Records held by local authorities, educational institutions, businesses or private individuals are not usually Public Records. If you need advice on whether the records you hold are Public Records contact your transfer advisor.

Records selection and appraisal

The process of evaluating your records to determine which should be transferred to The National Archives for permanent preservation, in line with the Public Records Act. Selection must be completed prior to using TDR.

Records transfer

The process of formally transferring custody of public records that have been selected for permanent preservation to The National Archives (or another approved Place of Deposit for public records).

Sensitivity review

Before you transfer records to The National Archives, you must review them to identify any sensitive information and apply the relevant FOI exemption(s). Transferred records will be released to the public except where FOI exemptions have been applied.

Series reference

A reference assigned to a set of archived records. Series references generally indicate the creating department and the subject matter of the records.

Transfer

Once records have been uploaded, checked and exported, the legal custody of the records is then given to TNA. At this stage, the records are not yet preserved and so the original records should not be deleted until the user is notified of preservation.

Transferring body

The government department or body that is transferring records for permanent preservation. This is often the same as the body that originally created the records.

Upload

At the start of the TDR process, a top-level folder containing files and sub-folders intended to be transferred to TNA are added by the user. These records are then checked to ensure they meet the correct criteria for transfer.
